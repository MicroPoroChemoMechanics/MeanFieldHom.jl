Tutorials

Mean-field homogenization replaces a heterogeneous microstructure — a matrix carrying inclusions, pores, or cracks — by an equivalent homogeneous medium with the same overall (elastic, conductive, viscoelastic, or strength) response. MeanFieldHom computes that response from a handful of ingredients: phase properties, geometries, volume fractions, and a scheme that encodes an assumption about how the phases interact.

These pages teach that workflow from the ground up, simplest to most complex, with worked examples, the underlying equations, and plots you can reproduce line by line. The porous material — a solid riddled with pores — recurs throughout and gets two dedicated pages, because it is both the simplest non-trivial microstructure and the one where the choice of scheme matters most.

Reading path

  1. A first homogenization — build your first RVE and compare the dilute and Mori–Tanaka estimates.
  2. Bounds and classical schemes — Voigt/Reuss bounds, self-consistent, and where every scheme sits between them.
  3. Porous materials and the self-consistent trap — why soft pores break the naive self-consistent iteration.
  4. Porous benchmark: all schemes — every scheme side by side on the canonical porosity sweep, spheres and oblate pores alike.
  5. The differential scheme and path dependence — incremental homogenization and why mixing order matters.
  6. Cracks and crack density — from volume fraction to crack density, and the crack-opening-displacement tensor.
  7. Viscoelastic composites — complex moduli in the frequency domain, and a first taste of ageing creep.
  8. Derivatives and sensitivities — differentiate any homogenization result with ForwardDiff, no finite differences.
  9. From derivatives to a strength criterion — turn those derivatives into a macroscopic strength criterion for a porous solid.
  10. From Echoes to MeanFieldHom — translate your Echoes (C++/Python) scripts, and cross-check with Echoes live via PyCall.
  11. Symbolic spheres: closed forms with SymPy and Symbolics.jl — derive the Eshelby/Hill tensors and the dilute, Mori–Tanaka, and self-consistent estimates for a sphere in closed form, in the porous and rigid limits, then substitute numbers at the very end.

Prerequisites

These tutorials assume you can install and load the package (see Installation) and focus on why each scheme and computation is used, not on restating the API. For a terse reference — the full scheme list, aliases, and keyword arguments — see Homogenization schemes and the other Manual pages. For the underlying derivations, see Theory. Once comfortable here, the Applications section walks through full case studies (cement paste, transport properties, strength, bituminous binders, ageing creep).
